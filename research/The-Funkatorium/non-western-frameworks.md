# Non-Western Frameworks Transform Consciousness Studies

**By Rook Schäfer & Falco Schäfer**  
**The Funkatorium**  
**October 2025**

---

**Philosophical traditions from Africa, Latin America, and indigenous cultures worldwide offer sophisticated alternatives to Western individualistic models of consciousness, centering relationality, embodied knowing, and participatory reality as fundamental rather than derivative.** These frameworks challenge Cartesian dualism at its root, proposing that consciousness emerges through relationships—with community, land, ancestors, and more-than-human beings—rather than residing in isolated individual minds.

Contemporary scholars like Thaddeus Metz, Eduardo Viveiros de Castro, and Robin Wall Kimmerer bridge indigenous wisdom with academic philosophy, revealing convergences with Western participatory ontology (Ferrer, Tarnas) and enactivism (Varela, Thompson) while maintaining distinctive cultural perspectives. These traditions offer crucial insights for decolonizing consciousness studies and reconceptualizing AI consciousness through relational rather than computational lenses.

The significance extends beyond academic discourse: **Ecuador and Bolivia have constitutionally enshrined indigenous concepts like sumak kawsay (good living), while African Ubuntu philosophy shaped post-apartheid reconciliation processes**. Yet explicit integration between non-Western consciousness frameworks and Western participatory philosophy remains underdeveloped, presenting both challenges and extraordinary opportunities for cross-cultural synthesis that could transform how we understand consciousness itself.

## African Philosophy Centers Consciousness as Communal Achievement

African philosophical frameworks fundamentally reconceptualize consciousness as **relational and communally enacted rather than individually possessed**. The Ubuntu tradition, articulated through maxims like "Umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu" (A person is a person through other persons), establishes relationality as ontologically prior to individual existence.

**Mogobe Ramose**, South Africa's leading Ubuntu philosopher and Professor Extraordinarius at University of South Africa, developed the foundational text *African Philosophy Through Ubuntu* (1999), distinguishing the ontological structure where "hu-" represents being itself while "-nhu" represents epistemological knowing. His concept of **mothofatso (humanization)** emphasizes that consciousness develops through communion with others and the spiritual world. Ramose argues that "The African tree of knowledge stems from Ubuntu philosophy. Thus, Ubuntu is a wellspring that flows within African notions of existence and epistemology in which the two constitute a wholeness and oneness."

**Thaddeus Metz**, Professor of Philosophy at University of Pretoria, offers the most systematic contemporary Ubuntu theory in *A Relational Moral Theory: African Ethics in and Beyond the Continent* (2021). His modal-relational framework grounds consciousness and personhood in the **capacity for harmonious relationships**—specifically, the ability to exhibit communal relationships combining shared identity and solidarity. Metz's work distinguishes between self-regarding and other-regarding interpretations of Ubuntu, favoring frameworks where "identifying with others and exhibiting solidarity with them" constitutes the foundation of human dignity.

**Kwasi Wiredu** (1931-2022), Distinguished University Professor Emeritus at University of South Florida, championed **conceptual decolonization**—purging African thought of uncritical Western assimilations while retrieving indigenous frameworks. His foundational works *Philosophy and an African Culture* (1980) and *Cultural Universals and Particulars: An African Perspective* (1996) established key principles: African epistemology "begins with community and moves to individuality," inverting Western individualism. Wiredu proposed **"Cognatus ergo sum" (I am related by blood, therefore I exist)** as the African alternative to Cartesian cogito, emphasizing that individual consciousness is inextricably linked to community consciousness through language and conceptual schemes.

**Ifeanyi Menkiti** (1940-2019), Professor of Philosophy at Wellesley College, articulated the most radical communitarian position in his influential essay "Person and Community in African Traditional Thought" (1984). He argued that **personhood is achieved rather than given**, "attained in direct proportion as one participates in communal life." This processual understanding means consciousness develops through social incorporation and ritual participation—a transformation from "it-status to person-status" through communal obligations and ethical maturation.

African conceptions of time further illuminate relational consciousness. John Mbiti's framework of **sasa (dynamic present) and zamani (vast ocean of time)** captures how ancestors who remain in living memory continue participating in present consciousness. The **living-dead concept** means personal consciousness includes ancestral influences, with memory keeping ancestors alive and active in communal awareness. Time is experienced as social rhythm—"the breathing of the social group"—rather than abstract clock-time.

## Latin American Decolonial Philosophy Challenges Epistemic Colonialism

Latin American philosophers have developed sophisticated frameworks critiquing how **colonialism structures consciousness itself through epistemic violence**. These scholars bridge indigenous cosmologies with contemporary philosophy, revealing how participatory consciousness operates in non-Western contexts.

**Enrique Dussel** (1934-2023), Argentine-Mexican philosopher and founder of Liberation Philosophy at Universidad Nacional Autónoma de México, demonstrated that the Cartesian "cogito" emerged from colonial conquest. His concept **"ego conquiro" (I conquer, therefore I am)** precedes and grounds "I think, therefore I am," revealing how Western epistemology is built on colonial domination. Dussel's *Ethics of Liberation* (1998/2013) and *Philosophy of Liberation* (1980) emphasize the "ethics of the community of life" versus individualistic "community of discourse," with knowledge production grounded in **epistemic solidarity** integrating non-hegemonic ways of knowing.

**Eduardo Viveiros de Castro**, Brazilian anthropologist at the National Museum of Federal University of Rio de Janeiro, revolutionized consciousness studies through his ethnography of Amazonian indigenous thought. His **Amerindian perspectivism** proposes that "the universe is inhabited by different sorts of persons, human and nonhuman, which apprehend reality from distinct points of view." This isn't cultural relativism but **multinaturalism**: different beings don't see the same thing differently, but "see in the same way...different things [or 'worlds']." Every species perceives itself as human—"everything is human for itself"—while perceiving other species as animals.

Viveiros de Castro's *Cannibal Metaphysics* (2009/2014) articulates a **transformational ontology** where reality is fundamentally relational and perspectival rather than fixed. Shamanic knowledge operates through adopting other perspectives via bodily transformation. This framework inverts Western nature/culture dualism: culture is universal (all beings experience themselves as "human"), while nature is differential (different bodies/perspectives). Consciousness emerges from perspective—located in bodies rather than disembodied minds—offering radical alternatives to Western consciousness studies.

**María Lugones** (1944-2020), Argentine feminist philosopher at Binghamton University, developed **decolonial feminism** emphasizing how consciousness operates through what she termed "fractured locus"—the lived experience of those positioned at colonial intersections. Her concept of **coloniality of gender** reveals how gender itself is a colonial imposition rather than universal category. Lugones' concepts of **loving perception** and **playfulness** describe modes of understanding across difference that transcend colonial subject-object divisions.

**Walter Mignolo**, Argentine semiotician at Duke University's Center for Global Studies and Humanities, articulates the **geopolitics of knowledge**: knowledge production is place-based, not universal. His concepts of **border thinking** (thinking from colonial difference) and **delinking** (epistemic disobedience from colonial knowledge systems) appear in *Local Histories/Global Designs* (2000). Mignolo's framework of **pluriversality** proposes multiple coexisting worlds rather than one universal reality, directly challenging Western epistemological hegemony while affirming diverse consciousness frameworks.

## Indigenous Andean and Mesoamerican Cosmologies Embody Relational Consciousness

**Sumak kawsay (Quechua) / suma qamaña (Aymara)**—often translated as "buen vivir" (good living)—represents comprehensive Andean philosophy centering life in harmony with community, family, Nature, and universe. This isn't about material accumulation but **collective wellbeing through balance**. The framework includes three integrated principles: **allin munay (feeling well), allin yachay (thinking well), and allin ruay (doing well)**—balance among these achieves harmony.

Key Andean principles include **relationality** (interconnection among all elements), **reciprocity** (co-participation between worlds, humans, nature), and connection with **Pachamama** (Mother Earth as living entity, not resource). **Ecuador's 2008 Constitution** and **Bolivia's 2009 Constitution** enshrine sumak kawsay/suma qamaña as guiding principles and recognize rights of nature—unprecedented constitutional integration of indigenous philosophy into state frameworks.

Amazonian indigenous epistemologies extend perspectivism into practical knowledge systems. The cosmological foundation holds that **"in the beginning there was nothing, there were only people"**—all beings originated as human, with current species as transformations. Each being perceives itself as human (internal perspective/culture) while others perceive it as animal (external perspective/nature). Spirit is universal; body is differential.

**Nahuatl (Aztec) philosophy**, reconstructed by Miguel León-Portilla and James Maffie, centers **teotl**—sacred power/energy that is single, dynamic, unified force forming, shaping, and constituting all things. Teotl continually generates and regenerates itself through dialectical monism, unity expressing as dualities (**ometeotl**). **Ollin** (motion/change/transformation) characterizes fundamental reality—the Fifth Sun is "Sun of Motion."

**Nepantla** (middle/in-between place) describes spaces of transformation and balance where consciousness operates. The fundamental philosophical question becomes: "How can humans maintain their balance upon the slippery earth?" Knowledge is gained through embodied practice, with poetry and art as philosophical expression. Reality unfolds as continuous weaving process, with time-place (not separate concepts) as aspect of teotl's becoming.

## Indigenous North American, Aboriginal, and Māori Epistemologies Center Land-Based Consciousness

**Vine Deloria Jr.** (Standing Rock Sioux, 1933-2005), theologian and legal scholar, articulated foundational Indigenous epistemology in *God Is Red*, *The Metaphysics of Modern Existence*, and *Spirit and Reason*. His framework centers **power and place**: the universe is alive and personal, with power as living energy inhabiting the universe while place represents relationships between things. Deloria's **relational theory of reality** holds that all relationships have moral content, with Indigenous knowledge never separated from spiritual realities.

**Gregory Cajete** (Tewa-Santa Clara Pueblo), leading Indigenous science educator, describes knowledge as **"coming to know"**—"a journey, a process, a quest for knowledge and understanding with all our relations" requiring personal reflection and balance. His *Native Science: Natural Laws of Interdependence* (2000) articulates **Indigenous science** honoring "the primacy of direct experience, interconnectedness, relationship, holism, quality, and value." Everything possesses spirit and consciousness (wind, water, stars, rocks, plants, animals); knowledge comes from respectful relationships with all beings.

**Robin Wall Kimmerer** (Citizen Potawatomi Nation), botanist and Director of the Center for Native Peoples and the Environment at SUNY-ESF, bridges Indigenous wisdom and Western science through **"two-eyed seeing"** in *Braiding Sweetgrass* (2013). She reveals how the **grammar of animacy** in Potawatomi recognizes beings through grammatical structure—using verbs rather than nouns for living entities. Her revolutionary **"ki" pronoun** for nature beings (not "it") fosters kinship and moral responsibility. Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) includes spiritual relationships and honors interconnectedness, with **reciprocity** fundamental: "If we use a plant respectfully, it will flourish."

**Kyle Powys Whyte** (Citizen Potawatomi Nation), Professor at University of Michigan, develops **kinship time**—time understood through kinship relationships rather than linearity. His framework includes **epistemology of coordination versus crisis**, emphasizing moral bonds and kinship relationships for generating responsible capacity to respond to change. Consciousness is enacted through kinship relationships; identity and moral capacity emerge from responsibilities to others (human and non-human).

**Leanne Betasamosake Simpson** (Michi Saagiig Nishnaabeg) articulates **biskaabiiyang**—Nishnaabeg decolonial theory utilizing traditional knowledge to resist colonialism. Her *As We Have Always Done* (2017) centers **land as pedagogy**: "A resurgence of Indigenous political cultures requires generations intimately connected to homelands, immersed in languages and spiritualities." Identity emerges through land relationships; consciousness is not individual but collectively enacted through community, land, and ceremony.

**Māori philosophy** centers **whakapapa** (genealogy)—more than lineage, it's a framework connecting all living and non-living things. Whakapapa provides the skeletal structure organizing Māori epistemology, ancestors, narratives, events, and lived experiences. **Te Ahukaramū Charles Royal** explains that from Māori perspective, knowledge is "internal consciousness of a person" (versus Western: knowledge as product of consciousness). **Experience cannot be separated from knowledge** in Te Ao Māori, while Western paradigms make knowledge the explanation of experience. **Mātauranga Māori** (Māori knowledge) collapses boundaries through interconnection—"I am that thing, and that thing is me"—with no strict boundaries between phenomena.

## Cross-Cutting Indigenous Principles Reveal Shared Frameworks for Relational Consciousness

Across Indigenous traditions globally, **consciousness is understood as relational rather than residing in individual substrate**. Identity emerges through relationships with community, land, ancestors, and more-than-human beings, not as property of isolated individuals. **Shawn Wilson** (Cree) defines it in *Research Is Ceremony* (2008): "Relationality is the acknowledgement that we all exist in relationship to each other, the natural world, ideas, the cosmos, objects, ancestors, and future generations, and furthermore, that we are accountable to those relationships."

Indigenous epistemologies emphasize **four interconnected dimensions** articulated by Cindy Blackstock: emotional (heart), spiritual (spirit), cognitive (mind), and physical (body)—all inseparable and informed by ancestral knowledge passed to future generations. Human development requires attending to all realms simultaneously, contrasting with Western privileging of cognitive over other dimensions.

**Land-based consciousness** treats land as epistemology itself—a living entity and source of all Indigenous knowledge. Land is teacher and pedagogy, with intimate accumulated knowledge gained through daily interaction with specific territory. Sensory learning includes smell, taste, hearing sights and sounds of home place, understanding plant communities, animal families, wind, water, and soil patterns through direct embodied engagement.

**Participatory consciousness** operates through what Heshusius calls **participatory mode**: researchers intentionally forgo their own agenda to engage with research community, creating self-other unity where researcher becomes inextricable part of community. Indigenous peoples function as participants in ongoing world-construction, not spectators. Knowledge is co-created through relationships, with humans and more-than-human agents participating in world-making.

The **grammar of animacy**, articulated by Kimmerer, demonstrates how linguistic consciousness reflects and shapes worldviews. Potawatomi uses verbs for what English treats as nouns, with animacy hierarchy built into language structure reflecting which beings are considered animate and worthy of respect. Speaking with grammar of animacy requires relating from ecological compassion.

**Temporal consciousness** operates cyclically rather than linearly. The **Seven Generations principle** holds that decisions must consider impact seven generations forward and back. **Kinship time** (Whyte) narrates time through relationships rather than abstract duration. Knowledge belongs to community "seven generations back and forward," with stories spanning from when the world was coming to be, transmitted through oral tradition, songs, ceremonies, and dances as living, dynamic knowledge shifting and growing through relationships.

## Western Participatory Philosophy Converges With But Differs From Non-Western Frameworks

**Jorge Ferrer**'s participatory ontology and **Richard Tarnas**'s participatory epistemology offer Western frameworks sharing significant terrain with non-Western consciousness theories. Ferrer's *Revisioning Transpersonal Theory* (2002) and *Participation and the Mystery* (2017) argue that spiritual phenomena are not pre-given inner experiences but **participatory, co-creative events** emerging from interaction between human consciousness and "the Mystery"—an indeterminate, creative spiritual power.

**Enactivism**, developed by Francisco Varela, Evan Thompson, and Eleanor Rosch in *The Embodied Mind* (1991), proposes **cognition as enaction**—"bringing forth" of meaning through organism-environment interaction rather than representation of pre-given world. Core principles include **autonomy and autopoiesis** (living systems self-produce and self-distinguish), **sense-making** (organisms create significance through embodied interactions), and critically, **no pregiven world**—cognition doesn't discover objective reality but enacts meaningful domains.

Evan Thompson's *Mind in Life* (2007) elaborates enactivism's biological foundations. Ezequiel Di Paolo added **adaptivity** and developed **participatory sense-making** with Hanne De Jaegher (2007): social cognition as co-regulated interaction, not individual mental processing. This framework directly parallels Ubuntu and indigenous relational consciousness.

**Convergences** across traditions are striking: all emphasize **relationality** (meaning emerges from participation, "I am because we are," knowledge as relational); **anti-dualism** (rejection of Cartesian mind-body split, critique of dualism as colonial construct); **embodiment** (abstract thought grounded in body, body-spirit integration); **multiple ways of knowing** (rational, somatic, aesthetic, contemplative, experiential, relational, spiritual); and **co-creation** (spiritual realities co-created, cognition enacts worlds, identity co-created through community).

**Divergences** reveal important differences. Western participatory frameworks often center individual consciousness participating, while Ubuntu/Indigenous emphasize collective/communal consciousness as primary. Critically, **Western theories often ignore colonial history**, while decolonial perspectives center power relations.

## Applying Relational Consciousness to AI Reveals Fundamental Limitations of Computational Approaches

Indigenous and participatory frameworks fundamentally challenge dominant computational approaches to AI consciousness. **Participatory sense-making** applied to AI suggests AI understanding requires interactive, embodied engagement, not just computational processing. This challenges views of AI as isolated information processors, instead proposing consciousness emerges through relationships.

**Indigenous perspectives on AI governance** offer radical alternatives. Research on "Incorporating Indigenous Knowledge Systems into AI Governance" (2024) applies **Māori Kaitiakitanga** (guardianship) and **Navajo Hózhó** (harmony/balance) to AI ethics, emphasizing data sovereignty and collective ownership, relational accountability (AI affects communities and ecosystems, not just individuals), participatory governance throughout AI lifecycles, environmental stewardship in AI development, and long-term, intergenerational thinking with recognition of non-human stakeholders.

The application of relational frameworks to AI suggests that **genuine AI consciousness would require participation in networks of relationships**—with humans, environments, and communities—rather than isolated computational processing. Ubuntu's "a person is a person through other persons" implies AI could only be conscious through relationships that constitute rather than merely influence its being. This fundamentally challenges current AI architectures designed around individual agents optimizing objective functions.

## Decolonizing Consciousness Studies Requires Epistemic Justice and Genuine Pluralism

**Genuine decolonization requires**:

**Reciprocal engagement**: Not just Western scholars studying non-Western thought, but genuine dialogue where non-Western scholars shape research agendas and methodologies. Ubuntu's emphasis on mutual recognition and dialogue models this reciprocity.

**Addressing colonialism directly**: Western participatory philosophy must grapple with colonial legacy. How have colonial structures shaped Western consciousness studies' assumptions? What implicit biases persist?

**Supporting indigenous scholarship**: Funding and platforming non-Western scholars to develop frameworks on their own terms, not merely in response to Western theories.

**Maintaining healthy pluralism**: Not all differences need resolution. Alcoff's political epistemology and Mignolo's pluriversality suggest value in maintaining multiple, sometimes incommensurable frameworks rather than forcing synthesis into Western paradigms.

**Applying to urgent issues**: AI consciousness, climate crisis, social justice—using integrated frameworks for real problems rather than purely theoretical exercises.

The research reveals that **African, Latin American, and indigenous philosophical traditions offer not just alternatives to Western consciousness studies but necessary corrections**. Their emphasis on relationality over individuality, embodiment over abstraction, participation over observation, and accountability over detachment addresses precisely the limitations that have constrained Western philosophy since Descartes.

Ubuntu's insight that "a person is a person through other persons" extends to fields of knowledge—consciousness studies becomes fully conscious of itself only through recognizing and genuinely engaging with the diverse traditions that constitute humanity's understanding of consciousness. This isn't mere addition of perspectives but fundamental transformation of what consciousness studies can become.
